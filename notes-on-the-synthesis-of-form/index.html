Notes on the Synthesis of Form

#

Christopher Alexander presents a tool to help designers solve complex design problems.

#

Design Problem

#

A design problem is defined by a set of requirements that need to be met. The interactions between those requirements is what makes the problem hard to solve.

#

When discussing a design problem we must talk about the forces that the context applies to the form.

#

System = Form + Context

#

The goal of the design problem is to attain Fitness between the form and its context.

#

If we could give a formal description of those forces, we would not have a design problem. What makes the problem difficult is that we don't fully understand the field of forces of the context. Understanding this field and making the form that fits it is really two aspects of the same problem. This is one of the reason that problem analysis is one of the most important phase of the design process.

#

In practice we find those forces through a negative process of identifying Misfits.

#

Alexander represents a design problem as a graph of misfits. The misfits that have a causal linkage will be connected in the graph.

#

#

Because the misfits are not all strongly connected, subsystems will form.

#

When a misfit occurs, the links may trigger the misfits directly connected to it. The loosely-coupled subsystems help prevent the misfits to propagate to the rest of the system.

#

The inner nature of the building process is what matters. If the process allows this adaptation to happen progressively subsystem by subsystem, the form will always fit the context. Otherwise the form will be arbitrary and sensitive to any change in the context.

#

The Unselfconscious Process

#

The process of the unselfconscious (traditional) culture is characterized by a lack of explicit rules. The builders learn through imitation and correction. The unselfconscious process can be characterized by two major forces.

#

First, the force of tradition. There are rituals and taboos associated to building habits. People will not change the way they build shelters unless they have a good reason.

#

Second, when a misfit requires adaptation, the people of the unselfconscious culture react immediately. The material is readily available. And people don't wait for the designer, they fix their own home.

#

Those two forces, tradition and quick feedback, define a process that generates well-fitting forms. The quick feedback ensures that misfits are fixed as soon as they occur, preventing multiple subsystems to be changed at once. The force of tradition prevent the other minor changes to be made in the rest of the system.

#

Such process does not require excessive creative abilities from the agents. People need only to fix misfits as they uncover them. Wrong fixes will not be repeated.

#

The Selfconscious Process

#

On the other hand, the selfconscious (modern) culture requires teachers to invent rules that can be used in school to train the future form builders.

#

The two major forces responsible for the generation of well-fitting forms in the unselfconscious process disappear in the selfconscious process. People no longer build or fix their own home. And the force of tradition disappears. Change becomes fashionable. But this disappearance does not explain in itself why the selfconscious process cannot generate good form.

#

Because of the complexity of the problem and the need for explicit communication, the selfconscious designers introduce concepts and categories to describe the problem and its solutions.

#

However, most of the time those categories do not correspond to the causal structure of the problem.

#

#

Worse, the designers lose the ability to change them. Once codified the rules are taught and most designers cannot see beyond them. Moreover, the forces that can easily be made explicit gain more weight than the ones that can't. The designer does not see the fit or misfit of the problem but adherence or deviation from his conceptual dogmas.

#

The Program

#

In order to avoid the subjective and arbitrary pictures made by the selfconscious designer, Alexander proposes to create a new picture of those pictures. A third-level of description in a sense.

#

This picture is formal and based on mathematical sets. The misfits will be represented by a set of booleans variables. Another set will represent the signed and eventually weighted links between the misfits variables. A negative sign indicates conflict and a positive sign indicates concurrence.

#

The advantage of sets over quantifiable variables is that we can represent requirements that we don't know how to measure (e.g. need for individual solitude).

#

By identifying clusters, groups of highly interlinked misfits, we can create a hierarchical structure. This structure is called a program because it directs the work to design a solution.

#

This decomposition of the problem follows the natural structure of the problem and not arbitrary concepts. We end up with a set of smaller problems that can be solved independently.

#

Once we have a proper decomposition of the problem, we can design a solution for each subproblem independently because we know that the coupling between the clusters is low.

#

These solutions are called diagrams of forces and show the mapping from the problem to the solution. Later those diagrams will become design patterns.

#

Then the designer can synthesize the artifact by assembling the diagrams in a bottom-up fashion.
